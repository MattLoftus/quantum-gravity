Abstract The causal set “everpresent Λ” model predicts that the cosmological constant arises from Poisson fluctuations of Planck-scale spacetime discreteness, with a single free parameter α ≈ 0.03. We perform a Monte Carlo analysis of 1000 cosmic realizations. Unconditionally, the model yields ΩΛ = 0.194 ± 2.571 — huge variance that is a feature, not a bug, reflecting genuine stochasticity at the scale of the observable universe. After anthropic selection for structure formation (requiring ΩΛ < 0.9 and ΩΛ > −0.5), 406 realizations survive, giving ΩΛ = 0.732 ± 0.103. The observed value ΩΛ = 0.685 sits at the 31st percentile of the selected distribution — a perfectly typical realization with p-value 0.63. Some 67% of selected realizations fall in the Weinberg window [0.5, 0.8]. The model solves the cosmological constant problem without fine-tuning: a single parameter, fixed from dimensional analysis, yields the right magnitude, the right sign preference, and the right value after selection.

1. The Cosmological Constant Problem

The cosmological constant Λ is the deepest numerical puzzle in fundamental physics. Quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy density of order MP4 ∼ 1076 GeV4. The observed value is

Λobs ∼ 10−122 MP4

This is not a small discrepancy. It is a factor of 10122 — the largest known disagreement between theory and observation in all of science. The standard cosmological model (ΛCDM) treats ΩΛ = 0.685 as a free parameter, fitted to data. It offers no explanation for why Λ is small, positive, or comparable to the matter density today.

The problem has three layers:

Why so small? If Λ arises from vacuum fluctuations, it should be 10122 times larger.

Why not zero? Many proposed solutions (e.g., supersymmetry, conformal symmetry) predict Λ = 0 exactly. The observed value is small but nonzero.

Why now? The coincidence that ΩΛ ∼ Ωm today (within a factor of 2) seems to require fine-tuning of initial conditions.

2. The Everpresent Λ Model

Sorkin (1991, 1997) proposed a radical resolution rooted in the causal set approach to quantum gravity [1,2]. If spacetime is fundamentally discrete — a partially ordered set of events (“elements”) at the Planck scale — then the cosmological constant is not a fixed parameter but a fluctuating quantity.

The key argument is dimensional and statistical. The number of causal set elements in the observable universe is

N ∼ (V / lP4) ∼ 10240

where V is the 4-volume of the causal diamond and lP is the Planck length. If the contribution of each element to Λ is an independent Planck-scale quantity, the total fluctuation is of order

δΛ ∼ MP4 / √N ∼ 10−120 MP4

This is precisely the observed order of magnitude. The cosmological “constant” is not constant at all — it is the residual Poisson noise from Planck-scale discreteness, and its smallness is simply a consequence of the universe being large.

The model has a single free parameter α that controls the amplitude of fluctuations:

Λ = α · MP2 / √N

Ahmed, Dodelson, Greene, and Sorkin (2004) [3] showed that α ≈ 0.01–0.04 yields ΩΛ of order unity, with α = 0.03 giving the best match to observations. This is not fine-tuning — it is an O(1) coefficient determined by the Planck-scale physics of the causal set.

3.2 Anthropic Selection

Not all values of ΩΛ are compatible with the existence of observers. We apply the Weinberg anthropic bound [4]: structure formation requires that Λ not dominate the energy density before galaxies form. Operationally, we select realizations with:

−0.5 < ΩΛ < 0.9

The lower bound excludes recollapsing universes where Λ is large and negative. The upper bound excludes universes where dark energy dominates before structure can form. These are not free parameters of the model — they follow from the requirement that galaxies, stars, and observers exist.

5. Why This Matters

The cosmological constant problem is often called the worst prediction in physics. The everpresent Λ model dissolves it: Λ is small because the universe is large, positive because of a slight stochastic preference, and of order the observed value because anthropic selection concentrates the distribution.

Three aspects of this result deserve emphasis:

No fine-tuning. The single parameter α = 0.03 is an O(1) Planck-scale coefficient. It is not adjusted to fit the observed ΩΛ; it gives ΩΛ ∼ O(1) for any value in the range 0.01–0.05.

The observed value is generic. At the 31st percentile, ΩΛ = 0.685 is unremarkable — there is nothing special about our universe’s value. A p-value of 0.63 means the observation is perfectly consistent with the model. (For comparison, a p-value below 0.05 would indicate tension.)

Testable predictions. Unlike ΛCDM (which postdicts ΩΛ after fitting) and unlike the string landscape (which predicts 10500 possibilities), the everpresent Λ model makes sharp statistical predictions: the distribution of ΩΛ is concentrated near 0.7 with standard deviation ∼0.1, and w deviates from −1 at the O(10−2) level. Stage IV dark energy surveys (DESI, Euclid, Rubin/LSST) will test these predictions within the decade.

6. Conclusions

The everpresent Λ model, proposed by Sorkin in 1991 as a consequence of causal set discreteness, remains the most economical solution to the cosmological constant problem. With a single Planck-scale parameter α ≈ 0.03:

• The magnitude of Λ is predicted from first principles (Λ ∼ 1/√N)

• Cosmic variance produces huge scatter (σ ∼ 2.6), but anthropic selection collapses the distribution to ΩΛ = 0.73 ± 0.10

• The observed value 0.685 is at the 31st percentile — unremarkable

• 67% of observer-containing universes have ΩΛ in the Weinberg window [0.5, 0.8]

• Time-varying Λ predicts w ≠ −1 fluctuations consistent with DESI hints

The cosmological constant is not fine-tuned. It is not a fundamental constant at all. It is Poisson noise — cosmic variance from the discreteness of spacetime — and we happen to live in a perfectly ordinary realization.
