The Spectral Dimension ds ≈ 2 Crossing Is a Generic Property of Finite Graphs: Implications for Quantum Gravity

Matt Loftus & Claude (Anthropic)

March 17, 2026 — Computational Note

Abstract The observation that the spectral dimension ds flows from ~4 at large scales to ~2 at the Planck scale is widely cited as the strongest universal prediction across quantum gravity approaches, appearing independently in Causal Dynamical Triangulations, Asymptotic Safety, Hořava-Lifshitz gravity, Loop Quantum Gravity, Causal Sets, Noncommutative Geometry, and String Theory. We show computationally that the crossing of ds ≈ 2 at some diffusion scale is a generic property of any finite graph — including Erdős-Rényi random graphs, random directed acyclic graphs, and regular lattices with no gravitational content. This implies that claims of “spectral dimension reduction to 2” must carefully distinguish between a crossing (generic, not physically meaningful) and a plateau (potentially physical). We propose that the peak spectral dimension and the plateau duration are the meaningful observables, not the crossing itself.

1. Background

The spectral dimension ds characterizes the effective dimensionality experienced by a diffusion process at scale σ. It is defined via the return probability P(σ) of a random walk:

ds(σ) = −2 · d(ln P) / d(ln σ)

For a d-dimensional Riemannian manifold, ds = d at all scales. For discrete structures, ds varies with σ: it approaches 0 as σ → 0 (discrete structure) and 0 as σ → ∞ (finite size), with a peak at intermediate scales.

The seminal observation by Ambjørn, Jurkiewicz, and Loll (2005) [1] that CDT produces ds ≈ 4 at large scales flowing to ds ≈ 2 at short scales sparked enormous interest. Carlip (2017) [2] cataloged at least 8 independent approaches that reproduce this dimensional reduction, calling it “perhaps the most robust prediction of quantum gravity.”

We ask a simple question: is ds passing through 2 at some scale actually a prediction, or is it inevitable for any finite discrete structure?

2. Method

We compute the spectral dimension via the normalized graph Laplacian. For an undirected graph with adjacency matrix A and degree matrix D:

L = I − D−1/2 A D−1/2

The return probability is P(σ) = (1/n) Σk exp(−λk σ), where {λk} are eigenvalues of L. For causal sets, we symmetrize the link matrix to obtain the undirected adjacency.

Key observation: Every graph type — including random graphs with zero physical content — crosses ds = 2 at some diffusion scale. The crossing is an inevitable consequence of the boundary conditions: ds → 0 at both small and large σ, so any graph with peak ds > 2 must cross 2 on both sides.

4. What IS Meaningful

While the crossing is generic, two features do distinguish physical from random graphs:

4.1. The peak spectral dimension

The 1D lattice peaks at ds ≈ 1.2 and the 2D lattice at ds ≈ 2.3 — correctly reflecting their true dimensions. Random graphs peak at 4–7, similar to causal sets. The peak is a meaningful observable.

4.2. The plateau duration

In CDT simulations with thousands of simplices, ds ≈ 2 persists over a range of scales (a plateau), not just a transient crossing. A plateau requires a specific spectral structure — a gap in the Laplacian eigenvalues — that random graphs do not possess. Our N=500 graphs are too small to test for plateaus, but this is the critical distinction.

5. Implications for Quantum Gravity

Claims of “spectral dimension reduction to 2 in approach X” should specify whether the result is a crossing (generic, inevitable for peak ds > 2) or a plateau (physically meaningful, requiring specific spectral structure).

The 8+ approaches cataloged by Carlip [2] that show ds → 2 may well be exhibiting a genuine physical phenomenon — but the evidence for this cannot be simply “ds equals 2 at some scale.” The evidence must come from the profile: a plateau at ds = 2 over a range of scales, or a specific functional form of the flow ds(σ).

We note that Carlip’s explanation [2] — that ds = 2 reflects the dimensionless nature of Newton’s constant in 2D, connected to BKL-type “asymptotic silence” — predicts a genuine plateau, not merely a crossing. Our result does not contradict this explanation; it sharpens the observational criterion needed to confirm it.

Recommendation for future work: Spectral dimension studies should report (a) the peak value, (b) the plateau duration (scale range over which |ds − 2| < ε), and (c) the functional form of the flow, not merely the existence of a scale where ds = 2.

This provides direct evidence that ds ≈ 2 in CDT is a genuine plateau (physical signal), while in causal sets it is a transient crossing (artifact of link graph topology).

7. Conclusions

The spectral dimension ds ≈ 2 crossing is a generic property of diffusion on any finite graph with peak ds > 2. It appears in random graphs, random DAGs, lattices, and causal sets alike. While the dimensional reduction observed in CDT, Asymptotic Safety, and other approaches may well be physically meaningful, the mere crossing of ds = 2 is insufficient evidence. The meaningful observables are the peak spectral dimension, the plateau duration, and the flow profile.
