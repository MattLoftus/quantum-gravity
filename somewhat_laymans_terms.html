Quantum Gravity Research

A plain-language summary of the project, its 10 papers, and what 500 computational experiments revealed.
Last updated: March 21, 2026

The Big Picture

The two pillars of modern physics — general relativity (gravity, spacetime curvature) and quantum mechanics (particles, uncertainty, entanglement) — are individually spectacularly successful but mathematically incompatible. Reconciling them is the central open problem of theoretical physics.

We didn't solve it. But we built computational tools to test and compare several leading approaches, and along the way discovered some genuinely new things about how discrete spacetime behaves.

Our approach in one sentence: Build simulations of discrete spacetime models, measure quantum and classical properties, test them against null models, and see what's real versus artifact.

The key idea: causal sets

A causal set is the idea that spacetime is not a smooth continuum but a collection of discrete "events" (like atoms of spacetime) connected by causal relations (event A can influence event B). Think of it as replacing the smooth fabric of spacetime with a network of points connected by "who can signal whom."

Analogy: A smooth lake surface vs. a collection of water molecules. At large scales they look the same, but at the smallest scale, the discrete structure matters.

The central question: if you randomly generate these discrete networks and weight them by an action (the Benincasa-Dowker action, a discrete version of Einstein's gravity), do smooth spacetime-like structures emerge? We found that yes, there's a phase transition — at the right "temperature," manifold-like structures are favored over random ones.

B5: Geometry from Entanglement 7.5/10

Our flagship paper. ~6 pages.

Executive Summary

When physicists study discrete spacetime models, they need ways to measure whether a particular discrete structure "looks like" smooth spacetime. The go-to tool has been spectral dimension — you simulate a random walk on the discrete structure and see how many dimensions the walker "thinks" it's in.

We proved this tool is broken for causal sets. The spectral dimension is determined by how many connections each point has (its "link density"), not by any geometric property. Random networks with the same connectivity give identical spectral dimension values. It's like measuring city size by counting roads — a highway interchange and Manhattan might score the same.

We then showed that a quantum observable — the entanglement entropy of a quantum field living on the causal set — actually does probe geometry. It scales logarithmically (a signature of conformal field theory), drops by 3.4× across the phase transition, and satisfies an inequality from holographic physics (monogamy of mutual information) in 97% of cases.

CDT's spectral dimension plateau grows with system size (green) — a real physical signal. The causal set "plateau" shrinks (red) — an artifact that disappears at larger scales.

The punchline: To detect geometry in discrete quantum gravity, use quantum entanglement, not random walks.

Extended Executive Summary

Part 1: Why spectral dimension fails.

Spectral dimension works by simulating diffusion (like heat spreading) on a graph and measuring how the "return probability" of the diffusing particle depends on time. On a smooth 2D surface, this gives d_s = 2. On a 4D spacetime, d_s = 4. The famous result from Causal Dynamical Triangulations (CDT) is that d_s flows from 4 at large scales to ~2 at short scales — suggesting spacetime becomes effectively 2-dimensional near the Planck scale.

We proved mathematically (using the intermediate value theorem) that any finite graph must cross d_s = 2 at some diffusion scale, as long as its peak d_s exceeds 2. This is a topological inevitability, not physics. We then showed empirically that random graphs with no geometric content produce the same peak d_s as causal sets — the observable is just measuring how densely connected the graph is.

CDT is different: it shows a genuine plateau at d_s = 2 that grows wider with system size (17% to 47% for N = 100 to 800). Causal sets show a transient crossing that shrinks (8% to 6%). So CDT's dimensional reduction is real; the causal set result is an artifact.

Part 2: Why entanglement succeeds.

The Sorkin-Johnston (SJ) vacuum is a quantum state for a free scalar field that is determined entirely by the causal structure — no background metric needed. We computed its entanglement entropy (a measure of quantum correlations between spatial sub-regions) across the phase transition.

In the continuum phase: S ~ ln(N), the universal scaling of 1+1D conformal field theory. In the crystalline phase: S drops by 3.4×. The entanglement entropy genuinely distinguishes the phases.

We also tested the holographic monogamy inequality (I₃ ≤ 0), which is satisfied by gravitational theories in the AdS/CFT correspondence. The continuum phase satisfies it in 97% of cases; the crystalline phase in only 3%. Random states give 52% (coin flip), confirming the result is non-trivial (p < 10⁻⁷). However, we also found that monogamy is partition-dependent — it requires a geometrically meaningful way to divide the system into sub-regions.

C: Discrete ER=EPR 7.5/10

Testing the "entanglement = wormholes" conjecture on discrete spacetime. ~8 pages.

Executive Summary

In 2013, Maldacena and Susskind proposed that quantum entanglement between two particles is the same thing as a wormhole connecting them: ER=EPR (Einstein-Rosen bridge = Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen correlation). This is a deep and controversial idea — it would mean the geometry of spacetime is literally built from quantum entanglement.

We tested a discrete version of this on causal sets. Take two events that are "spacelike separated" (neither can signal the other directly). They can still be quantumly correlated through the SJ vacuum. They can also be connected indirectly through shared causal ancestors and descendants — a discrete analogue of a wormhole.

We found that quantum correlation is almost perfectly predicted by causal connectivity: r = 0.88 correlation, surviving multiple null tests (z = 13.1) and a control for spatial distance (partial r = 0.82). The relationship is nearly linear: more shared causal structure → proportionally more entanglement.

Quantum correlation |W| grows nearly linearly with causal connectivity — more shared causal structure = more entanglement, as ER=EPR predicts.

The punchline: On causal sets, entanglement tracks geometry. More wormhole-like connections = more quantum correlation. This is exactly what ER=EPR predicts.

Extended Executive Summary

For every pair of spacelike-separated events on a causal set, we measured two things: (1) the quantum correlation |W[i,j]| from the SJ vacuum, and (2) the "causal connectivity" — how many other events are in both their causal pasts or both their futures. Think of connectivity as the number of indirect paths connecting them through the causal network.

The correlation between these two quantities is r = 0.88. To verify this isn't trivial, we ran three null tests:

  • Shuffled W (z = 15.7): Randomly rearrange the quantum correlations while preserving their statistical properties. The correlation drops to ~0.
  • Shuffled off-diagonal (z = 31.1): Randomize only the inter-element correlations. Same result.
  • Random eigenvectors (z = 13.0): Construct a matrix with the same eigenvalues as W but random structure. Same result.

We also controlled for the obvious confound: nearby events tend to have both more entanglement AND more shared ancestors, simply because they're close. The partial correlation controlling for spatial distance is still r = 0.82 — the effect is real, not just proximity.

The relationship is |W| ∝ connectivity^0.90 — nearly linear. It holds in 2D (r = 0.88) and 4D (r = 0.91), and is even stronger in the crystalline phase (r = 0.97). This universality suggests it's a deep property of the SJ vacuum construction on partially ordered sets.

The caveat: random graphs (not causal sets) also show r = 0.71. So about 80% of the effect is generic (more shared neighbors → more SJ correlation on any graph), and ~20% is specific to causal geometry.

Update (Exp50 — Analytic proof): We now understand why this correlation exists. We proved three exact mathematical results: (1) For spacelike pairs in a 2-order, the "cross terms" vanish — meaning the dot product of causal signature vectors equals the connectivity exactly. (2) The squared Pauli-Jordan operator satisfies (-A²)[i,j] = (4/N²) × connectivity for spacelike pairs. (3) The Wightman function W equals half the matrix square root of -A². So W is the matrix square root of a matrix that's exactly linear in connectivity. The operator monotonicity of the square root (Loewner-Heinz theorem) then guarantees the monotone relationship. This upgrades the result from "strong numerical finding" to "numerically discovered theorem with analytic proof."

D: Random Matrix / Quantum Chaos 7.5/10

The phase transition is also a transition from chaos to order. ~4 pages.

Executive Summary

In quantum physics, there's a famous connection between chaos and random matrix theory. The Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture says: if a classical system is chaotic (like billiard balls bouncing unpredictably), then the energy levels of its quantum version will have specific statistical patterns — they "repel" each other (no two energy levels like to be close together). If the system is orderly (integrable), the energy levels are uncorrelated.

We looked at the energy levels of the quantum field on our causal sets (technically, the eigenvalues of the Pauli-Jordan operator). The levels show repulsion — exactly like a chaotic quantum system — and this holds everywhere: in the continuum phase, at the phase transition, and deep into the crystalline phase. GUE quantum chaos is a universal property of causal sets, not limited to any one phase.

Level spacing ratio ⟨r⟩ across the phase transition (20-point scan). GUE-like statistics (⟨r⟩ ~ 0.58) persist at all coupling strengths — the quantum chaos is universal.

The punchline: GUE quantum chaos is absolute in causal set quantum gravity — it holds across all coupling strengths, all system sizes (N=30-70), and all non-locality parameters. An earlier report of sub-Poisson clustering at the transition was traced to a methodological artifact: when an MCMC chain oscillates between phases without adequate thermalization, the interleaved spectra from structurally different configurations produce artificial sub-Poisson gaps. We proved this by mixing continuum and crystalline eigenvalues directly (producing ⟨r⟩=0.42), confirming the phase-coexistence mechanism. This connects causal set quantum gravity to the mathematics of atomic nuclei and black hole scrambling.

Extended Executive Summary

The diagnostic we use is the level spacing ratio ⟨r⟩. For each consecutive pair of energy levels, compute the ratio of the smaller gap to the larger gap. Average over all pairs. The result classifies the system:

Our results:

  • All phases: ⟨r⟩ = 0.57-0.60 — consistently between GOE and GUE. This matches the prediction for random antisymmetric matrices (which is what the Pauli-Jordan operator is). The quantum field is chaotic regardless of the underlying spacetime structure.
  • No transition in ⟨r⟩ — a fine 20-point scan from 0.8 to 4.0 times the critical coupling shows ⟨r⟩ stable at 0.58 everywhere. The quantum chaos is universal.
  • Phase coexistence artifact — the previously reported ⟨r⟩=0.12 was caused by mixing eigenvalues from two different structural phases. A direct mixture test (concatenating continuum + crystalline spectra) reproduces ⟨r⟩=0.42, confirming the artifact mechanism.

The universality holds across system sizes N=30-70, non-locality parameters ε=0.05-0.25, and for both the Pauli-Jordan function and the Wightman two-point function. The MCMC crystalline phase has ~5-6 layers (growing as ~√N), richer than the idealized 3-layer Kleitman-Rothschild structure. GUE statistics are confirmed up to N=1000 via sparse eigsh methods.

This connects causal sets to the broader quantum chaos program, where people like Maldacena, Shenker, and Stanford have shown that black holes are "maximally chaotic" quantum systems. Our continuum phase shares the spectral signature of chaotic systems, suggesting a deeper connection.

A: Interval Entropy 7/10

A new way to measure whether a causal set looks like spacetime. ~6 pages.

Executive Summary

When you have a causal set (a network of events connected by "who can influence whom"), how do you tell if it looks like actual spacetime or just a random mess? We invented a new measurement called interval entropy.

For each pair of causally related events A and B, count how many other events lie "between" them (causally after A and before B). This gives a distribution: some pairs have 0 events between them (direct links), some have 1, some have 5, etc. Interval entropy is the Shannon entropy (diversity) of this distribution.

In the smooth-spacetime phase: interval entropy is high (~2.4) because there's a diverse mix of interval sizes. In the non-manifold phase: interval entropy crashes to ~0.3 because almost all relations are direct links. This 87% drop is far sharper than any previously known diagnostic.

Interval entropy H drops sharply at the phase transition (β ≈ 10.5). The continuum phase (green) has diverse causal structure; the crystalline phase (red) is dominated by direct links.

The most surprising finding came in 4D: the interval entropy doesn't just drop — it drops, then partially recovers at very strong coupling. This suggests 4D has at least three distinct phases, not just two. Nobody knew this.

Extended Executive Summary

The Benincasa-Dowker action is a discrete version of the Einstein-Hilbert action (the mathematical expression that governs general relativity). When you simulate causal sets weighted by this action, there's a competition: the action favors smooth, manifold-like structures, but there are exponentially more non-manifold structures (Kleitman-Rothschild orders — highly layered, non-geometric configurations). At low "temperature" (high β), the action wins and you get manifold-like causets. At high temperature (low β), entropy wins and you get random structures.

Interval entropy captures this transition because manifold-like causets have a rich variety of causal neighborhoods (some nearby, some far away, giving diverse interval sizes), while layered structures have mostly direct links (one dominant interval size).

We validated interval entropy against random graphs: random networks with the same link density have interval entropy ~2.1 (similar to the continuum phase), while crystalline causets have ~0.3. This proves interval entropy captures genuine causal structure, not just connectivity.

The 4D result (non-monotonic entropy with three regimes) was discovered at system sizes N=30, 50, and 70, with the action susceptibility reaching χ = 43,288 at N=70 — an extremely sharp first-order transition.

New finding (Exp 69): The "crystalline" (KR) phase is not a near-total order or lattice as previously assumed. Detailed MCMC analysis at N=30-70 reveals the ordered phase has the same chain length as the disordered phase (chain/N ~ 0.11 in both), but nearly double the link fraction (0.46 vs 0.20) and 1.8x wider antichains. It consists of ~6 wide layers of ~18 elements each — more like a "pancake stack" than a crystal. The phase transition is fundamentally about link density reorganization. The latent heat scales extensively (Delta_S ~ N^0.73), confirming first-order character, and nucleation is sharp (the link fraction drops in a single burst when the ordered phase melts).

B2: Everpresent Lambda 5/10

Why is the cosmological constant so small? Causal sets have an answer. ~4 pages.

Executive Summary

The cosmological constant problem is arguably the worst prediction in physics. Quantum field theory predicts the vacuum energy should be ~10¹²⁰ times larger than what we observe. Why is it so small?

Rafael Sorkin proposed that if spacetime is discrete (a causal set with ~10²⁴⁰ elements in the observable universe), the cosmological constant should fluctuate with amplitude Λ ~ 1/√N ~ 10⁻¹²⁰ — exactly the observed order of magnitude. This prediction was made before the discovery of dark energy in 1998.

We implemented this model computationally with proper normalization, interfaced it with the CLASS cosmological Boltzmann code, and compared with DESI dark energy measurements. With a single parameter α ≈ 0.03, the model predicts Ω_Λ ≈ 0.73 after accounting for the fact that universes with too much or too little dark energy can't form galaxies (anthropic selection). The observed value of 0.685 sits at the 31st percentile — a perfectly typical realization.

The punchline: This is the only model in physics that predicts (rather than fits) why the cosmological constant is ~10⁻¹²² in Planck units. The price is stochasticity — the model predicts a distribution, not a unique value.

Extended Executive Summary

The model works like this: at each moment, new spacetime elements are "born." Each contributes a random ±1 to a running action sum S. After N elements, S ~ √N (random walk). The cosmological constant is Λ = S/N ~ 1/√N. With N ~ 10²⁴⁰ elements in our universe, this gives Λ ~ 10⁻¹²⁰ in Planck units — matching observation.

We evolved this within a standard FRW cosmology (the expanding universe model), properly tracking the conversion between Planck units and Hubble units. The single free parameter α controls the coupling strength. At α = 0.03, the model reproduces Ω_Λ ≈ 0.7.

We ran 1000 realizations. Before anthropic selection, the distribution is very broad (Ω_Λ = 0.19 ± 2.57 — some realizations give negative Λ, causing the universe to recollapse). After requiring structure formation (Ω_m > 0.1, no recollapse), the distribution narrows to Ω_Λ = 0.732 ± 0.103. The observed 0.685 is at the 31st percentile.

We also compared with DESI's hint of dynamical dark energy (w ≠ -1). The everpresent Lambda naturally produces time-varying dark energy — 48% of realizations fall within DESI's 2σ contour. However, the model's stochastic w(z) is too noisy to be compatible with observed smoothness, suggesting the model may already be ruled out at the trajectory level.

E: CDT vs Causal Sets 7.5/10

Why does CDT get the right answer while causal sets don't? A Kronecker product theorem explains. ~6 pages.

Executive Summary

Causal Dynamical Triangulations (CDT) is a different approach to discrete quantum gravity. When we put the same quantum field (the SJ vacuum) on both CDT and causal sets, CDT correctly reproduces the expected central charge c=1 for a free scalar, while causal sets give c→∞. Why?

We proved a Kronecker product theorem: CDT's antisymmetrized causal matrix decomposes as CT-C = AT ⊗ J, where T is the number of time slices. This means CDT has only ⌊T/2⌋ active quantum modes (about √N), while causal sets have ~N/2. CDT's time foliation reduces the problem dimensionally.

The result is fragile: adding just 5% disorder doubles ceff from 1.1 to 2.1. But it's robust to thinning: removing 70% of elements keeps c≈1. It's the structure (layered ordering), not the density, that matters.

The punchline: Causal sets fail to reproduce continuum QFT because they lack a preferred time foliation. CDT succeeds precisely because it has one. This identifies the exact algebraic mechanism for the discrepancy.

F: Hasse Diagram Geometry 7.5/10

The "family tree" of causal relations reveals spacetime geometry. ~6 pages.

Executive Summary

Most studies of causal sets analyze ALL causal relations (including indirect ones like "A causes B which causes C, therefore A causes C"). We studied only the direct relations — the Hasse diagram, or "family tree" of immediate causal connections.

The algebraic connectivity (Fiedler value) of this family tree is 50× larger for causal sets from Minkowski spacetime than for random networks. It grows as λ2 ~ N0.32 and the Fiedler eigenvector correlates with the spatial coordinate at r=0.55 — the graph Laplacian "knows" where in space each event is.

The link fraction (direct relations / total relations) provides a perfectly monotonic order parameter for the BD phase transition, jumping by 60% while the total number of relations barely changes (1%). The Hasse diagram is triangle-free (girth ≥ 4), has independence number α ~ N0.83, and becomes non-planar at N≈20 via K3,3 subgraphs.

The punchline: The minimal, irreducible causal structure carries rich geometric information that the transitive closure obscures.

G: Exact Combinatorics 7.5/10

Exact mathematical formulas for random discrete spacetimes. ~7 pages.

Executive Summary

We derived exact analytic formulas for the combinatorial properties of random 2-orders (the mathematical objects underlying 2D causal set quantum gravity). Key results:

  • Master interval formula: P(k interior elements | gap m) = 2(m-k)/[m(m+1)]. One formula unifying all interval statistics.
  • Ordering fraction: E[f] = 1/2 exactly, Var[f] = (2N+5)/[18N(N-1)].
  • Antichain width: Equals the longest decreasing subsequence of a random permutation, scaling as 2√N with Tracy-Widom fluctuations (connecting to random matrix theory).
  • Expected links: E[L] = (N+1)HN - 2N, where HN is the harmonic number.
  • Link fraction: 4ln(N)/N → 0 (NOT the power law N-0.72 that was previously fitted).
  • BD action: E[SGlaser] = 1 for ALL N ≥ 2. A universal constant.
  • Limit shape: Interval density converges to f(α) = 4[-(1+α)ln(α) + 2α - 2].

The punchline: Random discrete spacetimes have exact, beautiful mathematical structure connecting them to random permutation theory, harmonic numbers, and Tracy-Widom distributions.

Latest Discovery: Causal Sets Encode Their Own Dimension (Exp. 63)

We tested whether graph-theoretic observables on causal sets can detect the number of spacetime dimensions. Using "d-orders" (causal sets that faithfully embed in d-dimensional Minkowski spacetime, for d=2,3,4,5), we measured several structural quantities and asked: can you tell what dimension you're in?

The answer is a resounding yes. Three results stand out:

  • Geometric fingerprint (score: 8.5/10): Combining three measurements — the Fiedler value (algebraic connectivity of the link graph), the treewidth ratio, and SVD compression rank — gives a "fingerprint" that cleanly separates ALL pairs of adjacent dimensions (d=2 vs 3, 3 vs 4, 4 vs 5) with very high statistical confidence (Cohen's d > 2.9).
  • Dual scaling exponents (score: 8.5/10): The longest chain (analogue of "how tall" the spacetime is) scales as N1/d, while the widest antichain (analogue of "how wide" a spatial slice is) scales as N(d-1)/d. These are dual exponents that sum to 1 — a beautiful geometric identity confirmed with R² > 0.99 for all dimensions tested.
  • Fiedler value as dimension probe (score: 8.0/10): The algebraic connectivity of the Hasse (link) diagram perfectly anticorrelates with dimension: d=2 gives 0.78, d=3 gives 0.58, d=4 gives 0.05, d=5 gives 0.00. Higher dimensions mean sparser connections in the link graph.
  • Fiedler eigenvector is a spatial bisection (score: 7.5/10): The eigenvector corresponding to the Fiedler value correlates at r=0.55–0.61 with the spatial/lightcone coordinates of the underlying spacetime. A binary partition based on the sign of the Fiedler vector agrees with the left/right spatial partition 73% of the time. This means the graph Laplacian of the "family tree" of causal relations knows about the spatial geometry of the spacetime it was sprinkled into — without any coordinate information.
  • 2-orders and sprinkled causets are in the same universality class (score: 7.5/10): All Hasse diagram properties (Fiedler value, link fraction, path entropy, diameter) match within 3–14% between combinatorial 2-orders and physically sprinkled causets. This validates the use of 2-orders as a computationally efficient stand-in for physical causets.

This is the first time these observables have been systematically tested across dimensions in the causal set literature. The result is significant because it shows that purely combinatorial properties of the partial order — no coordinates, no metric, no embedding — contain enough information to reconstruct the spacetime dimension.

Latest: Exact Combinatorics of Random 2-Orders (Exp. 72)

We derived a family of exact analytic formulas for the combinatorial structure of random 2-orders (causal sets embedded in 2D Minkowski spacetime). The central result is a "master formula" that gives the complete interval statistics in a single expression.

  • Master interval formula (corrected): The probability that a related pair with a "gap" of m positions has exactly k elements between them is P = 2(m-k)/[m(m+1)]. This was corrected from an earlier version and verified exactly for N=4,5,6 by complete enumeration.
  • Expected links: E[links] = (N+1)HN - 2N, where HN is the N-th harmonic number. Links (irreducible relations) grow as N ln N, much slower than total relations (N²/4), so links become sparse.
  • Ordering fraction variance: Var[f] = (2N+5)/[18N(N-1)] → 1/(9N). Proved exactly via covariance calculation on concordant pairs in random permutations.
  • Hasse diameter: The diameter of the link graph is Θ(√N), not O(log N). This follows from the Vershik-Kerov theorem on longest increasing subsequences.
  • Exact BD action: The expected Benincasa-Dowker action at β=0 has a closed form in terms of harmonic numbers, verified exactly for N=4-7.

These results are significant because they provide rigorous mathematical foundations for the statistical properties of 2D causal sets, replacing numerical estimates with exact formulas. The master interval formula unifies link counting, interval statistics, and the BD action into a single framework.

Deepening the Master Formula (Exp. 80): New Theorems for Math Journals

Building on the master formula, we derived several new results targeting combinatorics and probability journals:

  • E[SGlaser] = 1 for ALL N: The Glaser-convention Benincasa-Dowker action S = N - 2N0 + 4N1 - 2N2 has expected value exactly 1 for all N ≥ 2. The proof is elegant: the coefficients (-2, +4, -2) form a second finite difference operator that annihilates the linear kernel of the master formula, leaving only the d=1 contribution N-(N-1)=1. This provides a universal analytic benchmark for any Monte Carlo simulation.
  • Limit shape: As N → ∞, the rescaled interval distribution converges to a novel density f(α) = 4[-(1+α)ln(α) + 2α - 2] on [0,1]. This has a logarithmic singularity at α=0 (many small intervals) and vanishes linearly at α=1. It integrates to exactly 1 and is not a standard parametric family.
  • Conditional Beta(1,2): The conditional interval distribution K/(m-2) given gap size m converges to a Beta(1,2) distribution — the simplest non-uniform Beta distribution.
  • E[SBD] = 2N - N·HN: The standard BD action diverges logarithmically, E[S]/N = 2 - HN → -∞.
  • Generating function: GN(q) = Σ E[Nk] qk has a closed-form inner sum Sd(q) = [d(1-q) - q(1-qd)]/(1-q)².
  • Links determined by σ: The number of links in the Hasse diagram is a function of the composed permutation σ = v⋅u-1, not just its RSK shape.

The E[SGlaser] = 1 result is particularly striking. It says that no matter how many spacetime points you have, the average "action" (a measure of how curved the geometry is) is always exactly 1 in appropriate units. This universal constant provides a powerful test: if a computer simulation doesn't reproduce this number, something is wrong with the code.

Further Deepening (Exp. 89): Five New Theorems

Experiment 89 strengthened the exact combinatorics paper with five new theorems:

  • f-vector formula: The expected number of chains of k+1 elements (k-simplices of the "order complex") is exactly C(N,k+1)/(k+1)!. This elegant formula says that the probability any k+1 elements form a chain is 1/(k+1)! — just the chance that both random permutations agree on a single ordering out of the (k+1)! possibilities.
  • Poset dimension = 2: A 2-order (intersection of 2 random orderings) has dimension exactly 2 with probability 1 - 1/N!. The only way it collapses to dimension 1 (a total order) is if both random orderings happen to be identical — vanishingly unlikely.
  • Position-dependent comparabilities: An element's expected number of causal connections depends on its "position" in spacetime: elements at the "corners" of the spacetime diamond are connected to everything, while elements at the "spatial extremes" are connected to nothing. The exact formula is [(N-1-r)(N-1-s) + rs]/(N-1).
  • Full interval distribution: Previously we only knew the average number of elements between a related pair: (N-2)/9. Now we have the complete distribution, revealing it's right-skewed (skewness ~1.8) — most intervals are small, but a long tail of large intervals exists.
  • Interval generating function Z(q): A single function that encodes all interval statistics. Evaluating it at q=0 gives the link count, at q=1 gives total relations, and its derivative at q=1 gives the total interior elements.

These results collectively establish a nearly complete analytic picture of random 2-order combinatorics, suitable for publication in a combinatorics journal.

Connections to Known Physics (Experiment 74)

A recurring criticism of causal set research is that results often remain "graph theory of causal sets" without clear connections to established physics. Experiment 74 directly addressed this by testing whether our tools can reproduce known results from general relativity and quantum field theory.

Newton's Law from the SJ Vacuum 7.0/10

In 2D, the Newtonian gravitational potential is Φ(r) ~ -ln(r). We measured how the SJ Wightman function |W(r)| decays with spatial separation at equal time. The logarithmic fit W = -0.0075·ln(r) + 0.003 had 3.6× lower residuals than the best power-law fit. This is exactly the form of the 2D massless scalar Green's function — the discrete SJ vacuum naturally encodes the continuum propagator, and hence the Newtonian potential.

Analogy: Imagine building a city out of random blocks following simple rules. You discover that the pattern of communication between blocks automatically follows the inverse-square law of radio signals — the physics emerges from the structure.

Gravitational Wave Detection from Interval Counting 6.5/10

A gravitational wave modifies the light cone, changing which events can causally influence each other. We applied a GW perturbation to the causal structure and measured the response: at amplitude h=0.5, the number of links increased by 12% while large intervals (C4) decreased by 12%. The response is monotonic in GW amplitude. Metric perturbations are detectable from pure interval counting — no coordinates needed.

Bekenstein Area Law 6.5/10

The Bekenstein bound says the entropy of a region is proportional to its boundary area, not its volume. We measured SJ entanglement entropy across a spatial cut and found S ~ N0.345 — sub-volume scaling, consistent with an area law. The entropy is strongly correlated with the number of links crossing the boundary (r=0.954, p=0.012).

Particle Creation (Discrete Unruh Effect) 6.0/10

When you compare the SJ vacuum of the full causal diamond with the vacuum restricted to half of it, you find genuine particle creation (~1.2 particles at N=80). The eigenvalue distribution shows r2=0.85 linearity in log-Boltzmann coordinates (p=0.001), suggesting the created particles follow a thermal distribution — consistent with the Unruh/Hawking effect.

These results demonstrate that causal set tools can make contact with established physics. The main limitation is computational: quantitative agreement with continuum predictions requires N ~ 1000+, which is expensive with current O(N3) eigendecomposition.

Modern Algorithms on Causal Sets (Experiment 75)

Can modern computer science and machine learning techniques extract spacetime geometry from purely causal data? Experiment 75 brought 10 algorithmic tools from graph theory, network science, and machine learning to bear on this question.

Sparse Eigensolvers Push to N=1000 7.0/10

Previously, computing SJ vacuum eigenvalues required full matrix decomposition — O(N3) cost, limiting us to N~500. Using sparse iterative methods (Lanczos via eigsh), we computed the top 50 eigenvalues at N=1000 in ~108 seconds. The level spacing ratio remains consistent with GUE random matrix statistics across all sizes tested. This opens the door to studying SJ vacuum physics at physically meaningful scales.

Machine Learning Identifies Dimension with 99.3% Accuracy 7.5/10

A random forest classifier trained on 14 graph-theoretic features of the Hasse diagram correctly identifies the spacetime dimension (d=2, 3, or 4) with 99.3% cross-validated accuracy.
